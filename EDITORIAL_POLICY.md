# Editorial policy

## Scope

The handbook publishes research-aware, teachable and reproducible material for digital humanities. Text analysis is the main methodological spine, connected to data modelling, databases, GIS, networks, digital editions, multimodal material, AI, ethics and open research.

## Editorial layers

1. **Handbook chapters** are stable, synthetic and suitable for formal peer review.
2. **Learning paths** align selected material with courses or workshops.
3. **Workflows** solve one bounded practical problem and may evolve faster.
4. **Case studies** critically document public projects or repositories.
5. **Teaching materials** support classroom use but are not automatically part of a reviewed release.

## Acceptance criteria

A contribution must have a clear audience and research purpose; distinguish evidence from interpretation; identify inputs, outputs and assumptions; include validation or checking steps; acknowledge uncertainty, rights and ethical risks; use accessible language; and be reproducible from the information provided.

## Editorial responsibility

The editor of record decides whether a contribution belongs in the living edition and whether it is mature enough for a numbered release. Student work is never merged solely because it was submitted for assessment. It must meet the same review criteria as other contributions, and authorship must be credited.

## Corrections and withdrawal

Technical errors should be corrected through a documented pull request. Material may be withdrawn when it creates legal, ethical, privacy or safety problems. The Git history is preserved, but the public edition may replace sensitive content with an explanatory notice.
